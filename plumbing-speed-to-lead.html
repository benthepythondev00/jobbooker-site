Plumbing speed-to-lead: the job goes to whoever replies first

A burst pipe waits for nobody

Plumbing has the most time-sensitive inbound demand in the trades. Water moving where it should not be concentrates the customer's mind: they message two or three shops and book the first credible response. An enquiry answered tomorrow morning is a job that went to someone else last night.

Small teams have the most to lose

A one-to-three-truck plumbing shop is on jobs all day — hands full, phone in the van. The web form fills up exactly when nobody can watch it. Invoca's July 2026 benchmarks found only 52% of inbound home-services callers reached a person; for web forms, with no audible ring, the leakage is worse and invisible.

Response-time research points the same direction. A scoped historical InsideSales.com result found contact odds roughly 100× higher at five minutes than at thirty. Treat the exact figure as phone-era and directional — but "minutes, not hours" is the right mental model.

Measure before you fix

Export the last ten web leads and compute the time from each lead's timestamp to the first real reply — evenings and weekends included. JobBooker's free browser-only audit does this locally in your browser; no customer data leaves your machine.

What the fix looks like

The reliable version is a system, not a resolution to check email more often: every connected lead acknowledged in under a minute, emergency versus estimate triaged with your wording, flooding and sewage hazards escalated to a human immediately, real calendar slots booked inside your availability, and a weekly report of what was acknowledged, booked, escalated, or failed.
